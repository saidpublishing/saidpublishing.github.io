THE WORLD OF ËLDAMORIA

Imagine this: a manuscript, buried in the forgotten ruins of Ambar-Paladūr, lost to the world for nearly a thousand years. Then, just a few years ago, it was unearthed—its words like whispers from the past. But here’s where it gets interesting: this isn’t just another ancient story. What they found is something far more extraordinary, and they're calling it: The Chronicles of Eldamoria.

This trilogy is crafted in the vein of epic sagas, weaving a rich tapestry of cultures, conflicts, and characters that span a fantastical, yet familiar world called Eldamoria. It would be like a watered-down version of *A Game of Thrones* in its political intrigue, *Dune* in its futuristic placement with some groups having access to technology and others having minimal access, and *Lord of the Rings* in its plot that features an eschatologically inspired impending dystopia. There's perhaps a hint of C.S. Lewis in the trilogy's organization of place and time. However, it is global in its outlook, not ethnocentric, with characters from various parts of the world uniting to save Eldamoria from the King of the Seven Seas.

The book serves a readership interested in the future through the lens of today's political currents. In a world increasingly global, polarization grows, raising questions that modernity cannot always answer. The novel explores how small people with big hearts can change history, while those in power may find themselves restricted and unable to create a positive impact. While reminiscent of *Lord of the Rings*, *Chronicles of Narnia*, *Game of Thrones*, and *Dune*, this novel is more global and brings together diverse cultures and motivations. It reveals societies and people in their true form, unadulterated by modern systems, staying true to themes of the unity of Allah, humanity's purpose, and what is required to re-establish the caliphate as a defense against evil.
